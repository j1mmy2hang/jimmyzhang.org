## How we are doing it

Once we defined the first fundematal purpose of education as the impartation of knowledge, next we proceed to assess how and how well are we doing in this respect. Our current method to impart knowledge, as used in most traditional schools around the world, is undoubtedly lecture + testing. 

### Lecture

What exactly is happening in a lecture or class? To break it down to the very fundamental, it is someone --usually an adult hereafter referred to as the teacher -- trying to transfer the information & knowledge through speaking, with the assistance of simple information technologies -- powerpoint slides, blackboard, paper (worksheets), etc. 

At the meantime, a group of people -- usually young adults hereafter referred to as students -- sit together and try to receive the information mianly through listening, with some level visual assistance (wheter through looking at the slides and reading the worksheet in front of them). 

The teacher is challenged to transfer the knowledge from his head -- what he received through the curriculum training -- to the students, who are challenged to remember the knowledge in their heads. The main information technology used is spoken words. along with a certain level of written texts (on the slids or from the worksheet). 

In such a classroom, what often happens is the following: the teacher copies what's on the textbook onto the blackboard or copy-paste them onto the slides, and students copy whatever is on the blackboard or the slides onto their notebooks. Later, they will be challenged to remember what they've copied and apply them in tests desgined by whoever designed the curriculum. 

This is a very long way for the knowledge to travel through -- starting from a limited currculum to the limitation of spoken language. 

#### Limitation of Curriculum

Humans have come up with an unimaginably large quantity of knowledge since the beginning of our civilization. A large proportion of them are indeed outdated. And a small proportion of them are still developing and constatly being challenged. Those a curriculum will not cover. From the rest, a curriculum picks out the ones that he thins are the classic and important ones, and especially the ones that are easy to teach and tested upon. 

As a result great stuff and important stuff are often missed out -- logic, system dynamics, just to name two -- as the selection of course materials is an arbitruary choice that is subject to human bias. human bias is not scary -- what's detreimental is that there is no way to easily fix them and there are no or week self-correction mechanisms. Even if teachers or students realize that important parts are being missed out, they have no or little power to change. Teachers are tools of the curriculum to teach, and students are machines to learn. Changing the curriculum is an extremely imporbably task. And curriculums are updated once a year at most, and they seldom introduce large changes. 

Curriculum designers thought they are discovering truth about the world by handpicking those important knowledge, while in fact they are creating order. Students thought they are learning from the knowledge of previous human generations, however what they are actually learning is the curriculum's choices and the tests, submitting to the curriculum.

Second, inefficiecny. The way humans speak impost a detrimental limit on the amount of information that can be transferred -- just several bits per second, not considering noises or errors. One can only speak about 150 words a second. That's about 5 KB/min. Imagine downloading file at this speed, and you will know what I am talking about. No wonder it's no longer possible to learn everything. 

Passing down information through spoken words is an extremely old and outdated method of communication. Humans have been doing this since we were in the stone age -- young apes gather around a senior to listen to him tell his stories and pass down his experience of hunting by a campfire. And the same thing is still happening in our classroom in the thousands of years later. 

### Testing

Testing is already a widely-criticized subject. Yet it stands strong, for the one and only important role it plays: assessment. 

#### Assessment of what?

People say tests are assessments of how well students learn. (Later it will be argued that this should not be what tests are meant to measure.) However, in fact, tests are an assessment of the ultimate outcome of the whole process above mentioned -- but student learning is only the final phase in the entire loop. But people don't talk about test scores being an assessment of how well the teacher teaches, or how well the curriculum is designed. Tests can really assess the curriculum anyway -- tests are desgined by curriculum designers. They only test what the currriculum wants to test, which is everything but itself. 

#### (Not entirely) Useless & Harmful

Are tests useless? Definitely not. It provides insight for the teachers of how they taught, and for students of how they learned. Once students and teachers know where they are doing bad are, they can adjust accordingly and thus teach and learn better. How good is this! But obviously why isn't this happening?

Unfrotunately, this is not happening for two reasons. First, from the teacher's sides, teachers simply tend to blame the low test scores on their students instead of asking themselves. It's nutural to do this -- there are so many students to blame, why blame yourself?

Second, tests are muich more than assessments for students. They are not only an indicator of how well they learn -- they are the end product at the same time. Students learn to get that test score. That's just the grim reality. The end goal of learning is never-thought-of by most people and so ill-defined that the measurement and indicator became the end goal itself. And as Cambell's law states,corrupted metrics impedes the pursuit of goal. That's why people don't really talk about the impartation of knowledge of humanity's development. They talk about test scores. Yes, tests destorys motivation. An extrinsinc measure of success naturally replaces and destorys the intrinsic sense purpose and motivation. There is just nothing more to say about this. Intrinsic motivations to chase for an extrinsic measurement is no longer intrinsic, and harmful.  

Unfrotunately, this is the best and easiest way humans have designed to evaluate and assess their next generation in terms of how much they learned within a certain currciulum. And universities need them to select students, just because this is the only nuemrical metric they can rely on. Thus testing preserves. 

### Summary: Why Lecture + Testing Do Not Work

Yes, students can learn some of the knowledge previous human generations have developed through lecture and teaching. However, several important constraints predooms such apporach. 

1. The limitation of the curriculum
2. The limitation of human's spoken language (fallibility + inefficiency)
3. The limitation of corrupted metrics (replaces goal + destroys motivation)

If our goal is the impartantion of knowledge to the new generations, lecture + testing is not the way to go.
