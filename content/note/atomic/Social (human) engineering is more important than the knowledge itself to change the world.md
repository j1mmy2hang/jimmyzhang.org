Knowing how something works ≠ being able to change the world with that knowledge

Technical knowledge alone isn't enough to be a historical game-changer.
You need to become a master of human psychology and social dynamics.

## Resistance

### Instituional Inertia

- Existing power structures (guilds, religious institutions, monarchies) have vested interests in maintaining the status quo
- New technologies threaten established economic systems
- Academic institutions may reject ideas that contradict accepted theories

### Public Psychology & Fear

- People fear what they don't understand
- Electricity would seem like witchcraft or divine intervention
- Mass adoption requires overcoming deep-seated superstitions and cultural beliefs

## Changing the world requires changing the minds

Science discovers what's possible; social engineering determines what actually happens.

Civilization's progress isn't limited by our technical knowledge, but by our collective ability to:

- Communicate complex ideas simply
- Build trust and overcome fear
- Align incentives across different groups
- Create sustainable systems for change

This is why many brilliant inventors throughout history died poor and unknown.
This is also why successful innovators throughout history - from Edison to Jobs - were often better at marketing and social manipulation than pure technical innovation. 

The most brilliant invention is worthless if society rejects it, while mediocre technologies can transform the world if they're socially engineered effectively.

The real skill isn't just knowing how electricity works - it's knowing how _people_ work.
