The knowledge worker, as has been said again and again in this book, is rapidly becoming the major resource of the developed countries.

Only productivity of the knowledge worker can make it possible for developed countries to maintain their high standard of living against the competition of low-wage, developing economies.

The cohesion and strength of our society depend increasingly on the integration of the psychological and social needs of the knowledge worker with the goals of organization and of industrial society.

He is beholden to a knowledge area, yet he has to subordinate the authority of knowledge to organizational objectives and goals.

The knowledge worker is not poverty-prone. He is in danger of alienation, to use the fashionable word for boredom, frustration, and silent despair.

We will have to satisfy both the objective needs of society for performance by the organization, and the needs of the person for achievement and fulfillment.
