---
title: "Personal Statement"
---
I spent nine years in Chinese public school, one year at UWC Changshu, two years at UWC Maastricht. This fall I start at the University of Michigan. For most of that time, I thought the story was about pain — the pain of being a sensitive kid in a system that didn't want sensitive kids, and the slow work of getting out.

I don't think that's the story anymore.

The honest version is that I left Changshu after a year because I was afraid of being ordinary there. I spent two years at Maastricht convinced I had outgrown it, which was easier than admitting I hadn't figured out how to belong. I wrote a college essay about not needing school, and was surprised when schools agreed. Each time, I told myself I was escaping a system that couldn't hold me. Each time, what I was actually escaping was the possibility of a fair comparison — of staying somewhere long enough to find out who I am when the narrative of exceptional-kid-in-wrong-environment runs out.

I'm good at building things alone. I've shipped projects, taught myself what school wouldn't teach me, developed aesthetic and intellectual tastes I'm proud of. But I notice that most of what I've built is, in one way or another, a system for knowing myself without needing other people to tell me who I am. That's not nothing. It's also not enough.

The poem I wrote at seventeen asked me to remember the pain so I wouldn't lose myself. I'm starting to think the braver move is the opposite — to find out who I am after I stop carrying it as identity. I don't know yet what's underneath. That's the point.

Michigan wasn't my first choice. I've decided to stop treating that as the interesting fact about it. The interesting fact is that it's fifty thousand people, four years, and no exit clause I'm willing to keep pre-writing. I'm not going to spend freshman year drafting a transfer application to somewhere that would finally confirm what I want to believe about myself. I'm going to try, for the first time, staying.

I don't know if I'll be good at it. I've had more practice leaving.
