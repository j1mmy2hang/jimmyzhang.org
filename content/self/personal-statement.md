---
title: On Escape, and the first 18 years of my life
published: 2026-04-19
---
> "But Jonah rose to flee to Tarshish, away from the presence of the Lord."
> — Jonah 1:3

I spent nine years in Chinese public school, one year at UWC Changshu, two years at UWC Maastricht. This fall I start at the University of Michigan. For most of that time, I thought the story was about pain — the pain of being a smart kid in a system that failed to support smart kids, and the slow work of getting out.

I don't think that's the story anymore.

The honest version is that I left Changshu after a year because I was afraid of being ordinary there. I spent two years at Maastricht convinced I had outgrown it, which was easier than admitting I hadn't figured out how to belong. I wrote a college essay about not needing school. Each time, I told myself I was escaping a system that couldn't hold me. Each time, what I was actually escaping was the possibility of a fair comparison — of staying somewhere long enough to find out who I am when the narrative of exceptional-kid-in-wrong-environment runs out.

I'm good at building things alone. I've shipped projects, taught myself what school wouldn't teach me, developed intellectual wealth I'm proud of. But I notice that most of what I've built is, in one way or another, some sort of refuge — systems that let me operate without needing to be chosen, spaces where I define the rules so I don't have to be measured by anyone else's. 

Here's what I've been slow to admit: the world is mostly made by people, and by the games they design. Most of the things I hate most — credentialism, institutions that seem to run on bad faith, the feeling that the rules were written by unintelligent people — aren't bugs to be bypassed. They're the texture of shared life. And the people who navigate them well aren't the ones who found a purer system elsewhere. They're the ones who learned how to move inside the mess, to find what's worth taking seriously and build trust with people they didn't fully choose. I've been practicing the opposite. I've been very good at working alone on my laptop and calling it independence.

I once wrote a poem to remind myself to remember the pain so I wouldn't lose myself. I'm starting to think the braver move is the opposite — to find out who I am after I stop carrying the pain as identity. To find out whether I can actually learn something faster than anyone else, to connect with people I don't immediately understand, and to belong to something I didn't design. 

Michigan wasn't my first choice, but it doesn't matter anymore. What matters is that it's fifty thousand people, four years, and no exit clause I'm willing to keep pre-writing. I'm not going to spend freshman year drafting a transfer application to somewhere that would finally confirm what I want to believe about myself. I hope to try, for the first time, to stay and belong. 

I don't know if I'll manage it. I've had more practice leaving.

But there shall be escape no more.
