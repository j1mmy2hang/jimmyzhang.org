[[Effective decisions should be strategic & made at the highest conceptual level of understanding]]

The generic always has to be answered through a rule or a principle. 
One cannot develop rules for the exceptional

4 different types of occurences
- the truly generic
	- the individual occurrence is only a symptom - manifestations of underlying basic situations
- a unique event that is actually generic
- truly exceptional, the truly unique event
- the early manifestation of a new generic problem

By far the most common mistake is to treat a generic situation as if it were a series of unique events. That is to be pragmatic when one lacks the generic understanding and principle. 

> The effective decision-maker, therefore, always assumes initially that the problem is generic. He always assumes that the event that clamors for his attention is in reality a symptom. ==He looks for the true problem.== He is not content with doctoring the symptom alone. And if the event is truly unique, the experienced decision-maker suspects that this heralds a new underlying problem and that what appears as unique will turn out to have been simply the first manifestation of a new generic situation. This also explains why the effective decision-maker ==always tries to put his solution on the highest possible conceptual level.==
