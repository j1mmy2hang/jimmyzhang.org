School assignments are fundamentally industrial products that are at odds with the creation of art. 

## To create art is to make great new things. 

First of all, the standard of "greatness" is not defined by any one bur yourself -- you, through your own taste and judgment, decides what's good and what's beautiful, and pursue that greatness to the extreme. 

Second, "new" meaning something innovative and never done before. Great arts seek to express what's different about you with the world. We have seen enough similar works and repetitions. 

## To finish schools projects is to produce industrial products. 

First, the "greatness" of a product is not determined by you -- it's decided by some external organization and some power higher up. They give you the "criteria" and "rubrics". They demand to see certain charactersitics in your work, and forbid or ignore the rest. You have to conform to the rubric to be marked as "good". 

Second, industrial products are mass-produced. Especially in a school context, thousands if not hundreds of thousands of students have done the same assignment as you before you came, and thousdans if not hundreds of thousands of students will also do the same assignment after you leave. The format is usually set -- there is a template for a good work, and all good works look alike. 

In summary, you can do *well* on a school assignment -- as well as most other people. On the other hand, you can only achieve *greatness* when you are creating  -- as great as only you can do. 

## Possibilities of Recociliation

Most of the times, having to do school assignments is a torture for those who stay truthful to art and to their own heart. 

Some of the times, one can reconcile both by treating school assignments with artistic craftsmanship. I have always admired those who can reconcile the two with ease -- those are the winners both inside and outside the system. 

Inside, they deeply understand the rubric and what the institution wants from them -- and they give what they need, at the same time even being able to inject a little bits of art here and there, adding some surprises to the teachers who have to read the same work year after eyars. They harness high marks with both ease and joy. Outside, they also have the perfect capability to wield the freedom and pursue their own greatness. 

Bill Gate's school project on presenting the US State Delaware is a perfect example. 

> I inhaled everything I could find on Delaware. I trolled the stacks at the library, dug out Delaware, a Guide to the First State, and books on Delaware history, the state’s role in the Underground Railroad. I wrote to the state of Delaware for brochures on tourism and history. At home, Gami helped me cull articles from The Christian Science Monitor, Life, National Geographic, and The Seattle Times. I wrote to Delaware companies, including self-addressed stamped envelopes, and requested their annual reports. 
> 
> As I researched, I wrote. I wrote about the state’s history from the Lenni Lenape to the present, including a four-hundred-year chronology. I compiled a tourist’s guide to Wilmington and a history of the quaint historical town of Arden. I put together fictitious accounts of the lives of a Delawarean oyster fisherman and a granite miner. For good measure, I wrote a book report on Elin’s Amerika, the story of a young girl in seventeenth-century Delaware. I spent a lot of time researching the Delaware company DuPont. I wrote about the company’s management structure; I noted that its board was all men and mostly insiders. I detailed DuPont’s products, its overseas operations, and its research and development, and summarized the story of nylon’s invention, complete with the best descriptions I could muster on the chemistry of polymerization. I wrote up an obit of a board member who had worked his way from low-level salesman to executive committee member. 
> 
> By the time I was done, I had generated 177 pages on little Delaware. It’s hard to describe the pride I felt in that crazy-long report. I even fabricated a cover for it out of wood. It was in every way a dream assignment. In the privacy of my room, away from the judging eyes of other kids, I could do what I liked the most: read, collect facts, and synthesize information. The class jester wasn’t expected to turn in a tome. I liked seeing other kids’ confusion and admiration. My teacher loved it.


## Bottom Line, and The Path to Greatness

Neverthelss, the fundamental tension remains: school trains you to be a competent producer, but greatness requires you to be an original creator. You can put artistic craftsmanship into school projects so that the process is more enjoyable. 

But fundamentally, one should choose to create art whenever possible, and art always comes first. 

Here and now, I call all creative minds to action -- create art if possible. Arts are marked by their freedom -- freedom from judgments and freedom from rubrics. 

Arts are arts not because they are good according to anyone's standards, but because they (re)define what is good themselves. 

And art is the only path to greatness.
